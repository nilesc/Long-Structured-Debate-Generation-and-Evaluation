Discussion Title: Should the US have withdrawn from NAFTA?

1. It would have been in the interests of the US to withdraw from NAFTA.
1.1. Con: Withdrawing from NAFTA would have been harmful to the US economy.
1.1.1. Pro: It is estimated that [300,000](https://money.cnn.com/2018/01/16/news/economy/trump-nafta-withdraw-impact/index.html) US jobs would have been lost as a result of leaving NAFTA.
1.1.1.1. Pro: As many as [six million](http://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/naftas-impact-u-s-economy-facts/) US jobs are dependent on trade with Mexico. NAFTA has been instrumental in facilitating this.
1.1.1.2. Pro: The US textile industry is [highly reliant](http://tradeleadershipcoalition.org/nafta-is-critical-to-the-success-of-u-s-textiles/) on NAFTA's continued existence. In this industry, the US enjoys a bilateral trade surplus of $4 billion.
1.1.2. Con: Uncertainty over whether NAFTA will last or not prevents companies from forming long term trade plans. It is better to withdraw from NAFTA, so that companies can know which rules they have to trade over, rather than constantly fight over an agreement that may end in a few months.
1.1.2.1. Con: A strong commitment to remain in NAFTA would resolve this uncertainty more effectively than a decision to withdraw, which would leave the future terms of trade unclear, just as in the case of [Brexit](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2018/jun/26/brexit-uncertainty-putting-860000-jobs-at-risk-warns-car-industry).
1.1.2.1.1. Con: The confusion and uncertainly surrounding Brexit was, to a large extent, [caused](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2018/mar/02/theresa-may-brexit-mess) by the UK Government. Leaving NAFTA would allow the US government the opportunity to provide certainty as to what will happen next.
1.1.2.1.1.1. Con: No one would know what would come next, causing uncertainty in the short term, particularly from investors.
1.1.2.1.1.2. Con: The US cannot simply avoid uncertainty with a firm decision. Withdrawal from NAFTA would necessitate a lengthy negotiation period regarding future trade arrangements, during which time uncertainty would severely disrupt markets.
1.1.2.1.1.2.1. Con: NAFTA requires a [6 month](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/12/business/economy/what-would-happen-if-the-us-withdrew-from-nafta.html) period to notify the other parties of any plan to withdraw, allowing time for NAFTA to remain in place while a new agreement is being made. This would reduce the harms of uncertainty.
1.1.2.1.1.2.1.1. Con: Six months is very little time to negotiate a complex multilateral trade arrangement. [Brexit](http://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2018/06/06/britain-continues-to-make-a-mess-of-brexit-but-the-eu-has-mishandled-it-too/) has been underway for more than two years and no deal has been reached.
1.1.2.2. Pro: Uncertainty around renegotiations would hit Mexico even more [severely](https://www.voanews.com/a/mexico-gdp-shrinks-nafta-uncertainty-disasters/4094325.html) than the US. This would increase the number of people forced out of Mexico due to poor economic conditions.
1.1.2.2.1. Con: The 0.2% drop in Mexico's GDP cited in the above article is not necessarily correlated with uncertainty over NAFTA. The fact that industries such as [car manufacturing](https://www.seattletimes.com/nation-world/uncertainty-over-nafta-hasnt-stopped-mexicos-car-manufacturing-cities-from-thriving/?utm_source=RSS&utm_medium=Referral&utm_campaign=RSS_all), which are most reliant on NAFTA, are thriving indicates that the effect of uncertainty is minimal.
1.1.2.2.2. Pro: The peso sank [1.5%](https://www.ft.com/content/9fd58328-b04e-11e8-8d14-6f049d06439c) upon negative news about the future of NAFTA emerging. This will become worse the greater the uncertainty.
1.1.3. Pro: Leaving NAFTA would reduce America's GDP by [0.5%](https://www.ft.com/content/b0f343aa-ab3a-3657-8b57-f0086cfaa006), costing billions of dollars.
1.1.3.1. Con: GDP is an increasingly [inaccurate](https://www.economist.com/briefing/2016/04/30/the-trouble-with-gdp) measure of an economies prosperity. A reduction in GDP may not be as damning as it appears.
1.1.3.1.1. Con: While GDP is not a perfect measure of prosperity, it does indicate whether an economy is growing or shrinking. If one variable can be shown to shrink the economy, it is very likely it will have other harmful effects.
1.1.4. Pro: Immigration is a valuable driver of economic growth.
1.1.4.1. Pro: [Eight](https://theconversation.com/why-care-about-undocumented-immigrants-for-one-thing-theyve-become-vital-to-key-sectors-of-the-us-economy-98790) million of the estimated eleven million illegal immigrants in the U.S. currently work and contribute to the US economy.
1.1.4.1.1. Con: Leaving NAFTA won't stop illegal immigrants entering the US or working there illegally.
1.1.4.2. Pro: NAFTA facilitates the immigration of Mexican and Canadian professionals and investors through the TN and E [visas](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/employment/visas-canadian-mexican-nafta-professional-workers.html) respectively.
1.1.4.3. Pro: Immigration facilitated by NAFTA is essential for America's healthcare system. TN visas allow Canadian and Mexican healthcare professionals to commute cross-border easily. [\(Walker, pp.1-2\)](https://nfap.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/TN-Visas.NFAP-Policy-Brief.April-2018.pdf)
1.1.4.3.1. Con: It is possible to formulate immigration exemptions for specific highly valuable professions outside of the framework of NAFTA.
1.1.4.4. Pro: A high level of low-skilled migrant workers from Mexico is beneficial for the American economy.
1.1.4.4.1. Con: The fact that a certain level is helpful does not mean that an uncontrolled amount of illegal immigration is helpful to the US economy.
1.1.4.4.1.1. Pro: NAFTA has led to an increase in illegal immigration from Mexico.
1.1.4.4.1.1.1. Con: America is economically much better off than Mexico; there will always be illegal immigration regardless of NAFTA.
1.1.4.4.1.1.2. Pro: The inequality in agricultural subsidies and free trade in agricultural commodities has led to financial loss of Mexican agricultural workers in Mexico, forcing them to illegally emigrate to America in order to find employment \([Luckstead, 2](http://www.eldis.org/document/A61876)\).
1.1.4.4.1.1.2.1. Pro: [Unlike predictions](https://www.nytimes.com/2007/02/18/weekinreview/18uchitelle.html) before NAFTA was implemented, Mexican farmers did not diversify from corn to other grains or fruits.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3. Con: The [financial crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexican_peso_crisis) and devaluation of the peso, unrelated to the implementation of NAFTA, caused the increase in illegal immigration \([Papademetriou, 40](https://www.migrationpolicy.org/research/naftas-promise-and-reality-lessons-mexico-hemisphere)\).
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1. Pro: If US withdrawal from NAFTA [devastates](https://www.forbes.com/sites/kenrapoza/2017/10/31/nafta-exit-way-worse-for-mexico-than-u-s/) the Mexican economy, it could fuel more illegal immigration to the US.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1. Pro: A US withdrawal from NAFTA would have devastating effects on the Mexican economy.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.1. Con: Only [2.4%](https://fpif.org/5-reasons-mexican-workers-cheer-demise-nafta/) of Mexican jobs depend on exports.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2. Pro: [Oxford Economics](https://www.ft.com/content/b0f343aa-ab3a-3657-8b57-f0086cfaa006) estimates the value of the Peso would fall by 8%.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.1. Con: A devaluation of the peso could benefit the Mexican economy by [reducing](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/090215/3-reasons-why-countries-devalue-their-currency.asp) interest rates on sovereign debt repayments.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.1.1. Pro: 85% of Mexico's debt is denominated in pesos, and the cost of interest on all of this debt would [decline](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-mexico-debt-idUSKBN16V0J5) as the value of the peso did.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.2. Pro: A devaluation of the peso could cause regional financial instability throughout Central America, [as happened](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/tequilaeffect.asp) when the peso's value collapsed in the mid-1990s.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.2.1. Con: The mid-1990's crisis was induced by a nearly 50% devaluation of the Peso. An 8% dip, as predicted above, would not have the same negative effects.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.3. Con: A devaluation of the peso would reduce the price of Mexican goods abroad, helping to boost trade.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.3.1. Con: This devaluation would also make imports more expensive for Mexican citizens, making goods and services more expensive.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.3.1.1. Pro: Mexico's largest import is [electrical machinery](http://www.worldstopexports.com/mexicos-top-10-imports/): an increase in the cost could therefore harm businesses which rely on information or manufacturing technology, negatively impacting domestic output.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.2.3.1.2. Pro: As Mexico runs an overall [trade deficit](https://tradingeconomics.com/mexico/balance-of-trade), this effect would be more harmful than the benefits derived from cheap exports.

1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.3. Pro: It is predicted that the Mexican economy would [shrink](https://www.ft.com/content/b0f343aa-ab3a-3657-8b57-f0086cfaa006) by 2% by 2022 and it's growth in 2019 would be reduced by 0.9 percentage points.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.4. Pro: Many companies such as [Ford](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-03-30/what-might-happen-to-mexico-if-nafta-gets-wiped-away-quicktake) that use Mexico to manufacture goods for export to the US will choose to move the factories elsewhere out of fears of high tariffs.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.5. Con: Tariff free produce from the US has decimated [5 million](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-03-30/what-might-happen-to-mexico-if-nafta-gets-wiped-away-quicktake) agriculture jobs in Mexico; without NAFTA, Mexican producers would find it easier to stay profitable, employing more people.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.5.1. Con: Foreign competition only causes job loss when domestic production is too inefficient to compete. The removal of US competition would lead to the re-emergence of wasteful and inefficient agricultural practices in Mexico and would not be of benefit to the overall economy.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.5.1.1. Con: US agricultural produce is not cheap because it is produced more efficiently than in Mexico, but rather because it is [subsidised](https://www.economist.com/united-states/2015/02/12/milking-taxpayers) to a huge extent by the state. This prevents Mexican farmers from competing fairly.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.5.1.2. Con: Mexico is party to a [large number](http://www.promexico.gob.mx/en/mx/tratados-comerciales) of other free trade agreements, and domestic producers would therefore still have to contend with foreign competition.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.5.2. Pro: The 2002 Farm Bill subsidised US crops by as much as [40%](https://www.thebalance.com/disadvantages-of-nafta-3306273). NAFTA's removal of tariffs has made it impossible for Mexican farmers to compete.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.5.3. Con: For these businesses to be profitable, they would have to charge higher prices for these goods than those offered through NAFTA, making it more expensive for consumers.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.6. Pro: [75%](https://www.forbes.com/sites/kenrapoza/2017/10/31/nafta-exit-way-worse-for-mexico-than-u-s/#38bef1914da0) of Mexico's exports are to US consumers.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.6.1. Con: Only [50%](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/what-might-happen-to-mexico-if-nafta-gets-wiped-away-quicktake/2018/04/02/bc15962a-3684-11e8-af3c-2123715f78df_story.html?utm_term=.eb59557d9807) of these exports are included in NAFTA.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.7. Con: NAFTA has seen the growth of [Export Processing Zones](https://www.thebalancesmb.com/export-processing-zones-epz-2221273) where US companies locate near the boarder, hire Mexican workers and immediately ship goods back for sale in the U.S. Workers employed in this way have no labour rights or health [protections](https://www.thebalance.com/disadvantages-of-nafta-3306273).
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.7.1. Pro: NAFTA has lead to as many as [30%](https://www.thebalance.com/disadvantages-of-nafta-3306273) of the Mexican workforce being employed in these conditions.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.7.2. Con: Even if these workers do not have labour rights or health protections, they still have jobs that would be lost if the US left NAFTA.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.7.2.1. Con: Since EPZs have heavy tax exemptions, they use large amounts of Mexican labour and resources while channeling the proceeds out of the country. More jobs might be created in Mexico if the proceeds of Mexican industry were allowed to circulate in the domestic economy.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.7.2.2. Con: The majority of the benefits of EPZs flow to higher income workers. Those on lower incomes see very few benefits to either employment status or wages \([Picarelli, 320](https://ac.els-cdn.com/S0927537116300306/1-s2.0-S0927537116300306-main.pdf?_tid=d1ffdcc9-5f7b-4733-900f-81c5fd541914&acdnat=1537024037_a795e4b9da819d6f3d599b34a89f80ab)\).
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.7.3. Pro: Even where protections exist for workers in EPZs, they are easier to [flout](https://www.just-style.com/analysis/are-mexicos-maquila-labour-rights-worsening_id128988.aspx).
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.8. Con: Mexico has other trade agreements it can rely on.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.8.1. Pro: Mexico is in [negotiations](http://ec.europa.eu/trade/policy/in-focus/eu-mexico-trade-agreement/) for a trade deal with the EU.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.8.1.1. Con: EU-Mexico trade cannot be a substitute for US-Mexico trade. The distance between Mexico and the EU makes for substantially higher transport costs, limiting the profitability of transactions.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.8.2. Pro: Mexico recently signed a new [Trans-Pacific Partnership](https://foreignpolicy.com/2018/01/29/nafta-talks-stalled-as-world-moves-on-without-u-s-lighthizer-freeland/) with nine other Pacific Rim nations including Canada which would offer alternative trade partners for Mexico.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.8.2.1. Con: The spending power of Latin American countries has [fallen](https://www.ft.com/content/d3272242-57d5-11e5-a28b-50226830d644) greatly recently meaning they will have less money to buy Mexican goods.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.8.3. Con: Those trade agreements are not mutually exclusive to NAFTA: in no scenario is the economic situation improved for Mexico without it.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.9. Con: Although employment has increased, this has not seen a rise in wage growth for the poorest, and has therefore increased inequality, meaning ordinary Mexicans have [not seen the benefits](http://www.nber.org/digest/sep03/w9563.html) of NAFTA.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.9.1. Con: [Studies](https://fas.org/sgp/crs/row/R42965.pdf) have shown that NAFTA has increased productivity and reduced consumer prices in Mexico. These processes are of overall benefit to consumers and the economy even if the agreement has not increased wages.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.9.2. Pro: This has increased wage inequality and harmed low skilled workers. It is wrong to allow some to benefit at the expense of poorer workers. [\(Nicita 2004, 2\)](http://documents.worldbank.org/curated/en/491461468757218477/pdf/wps3265Mexico.pdf)
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.9.3. Pro: US companies that have entered Mexico since NAFTA's implementation have pushed for a lower standard of [labour rights](http://prospect.org/article/nafta-cross-border-disaster), reducing the quality of life for Mexican workers.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10. Con: The purpose of US trade policy is to vindicate American economic interests, not to ensure the health of the Mexican economy.

1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.1. Pro: Financial crises in Mexico have been linked to an [increase](https://www.migrationpolicy.org/research/naftas-promise-and-reality-lessons-mexico-hemisphere) in illegal immigration into the United States.

Pro: The [volatility](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/these-7-trigger-points-turned-stock-market-hope-into-fear-since-the-election-2018-04-12) of the US stock market hit an all time high in 2018. Con: The effect of political scandal is largely [confined](https://www.cnbc.com/2018/08/24/a-trump-impeachment-could-slam-stock-market-but-not-economy.html) to short term volatility in the stock market, which has little to do with the more important long term trajectory of the economy.

They are likely to continue to do so in order to win votes regardless of actual immigration levels.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.1.1.1.4. Con: The American economy is performing [strongly](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/business-42748243) under Trump, with falling unemployment and high levels of growth.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.1.1.2. Pro: Illegal immigration correlates with a [decrease](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2018/03/30/upshot/crime-immigration-myth.html) in crime, because immigrants face the deterrent of deportation.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.1.1.3. Pro: Undocumented immigrants pay [substantial](https://www.wnycstudios.org/story/undocumented-immigrants-and-their-contributions-tax-revenue/) amounts of tax while having limited access to public services, meaning they may contribute more to the economy than they cost.

Con: Although access to some services is limited, illegal immigrants do still benefit to varying extents from services such as water, police, rubbish collection, and road maintenance. Facilitating them is estimated to cost [$134.9 billion](https://fairus.org/issue/publications-resources/fiscal-burden-illegal-immigration-united-states-taxpayers) annually.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.1.1.3.1.1. Pro: The medical costs for providing emergency treatment to uninsured illegal immigrants amounts to as much as [3.9 billion](https://www.forbes.com/sites/theapothecary/2018/02/26/how-american-citizens-finance-health-care-for-undocumented-immigrants/#685dbe3612c4) every year.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.1.1.4. Pro: Undocumented workers are often [willing](https://www.brookings.edu/essay/the-wall-the-real-costs-of-a-barrier-between-the-united-states-and-mexico/) to take on difficult or unpleasant jobs which citizens and documented immigrants are unwilling to do.
1.1.4.4.1.1.3.1.1.10.1.2. Pro: Unemployment and economic desperation in Mexico contribute to the [growth](https://www.alternet.org/drugs/mexico-trapped-labor-force-fertile-recruiting-ground-drug-cartels) of violent cartels by providing them with a labour force.

1.1.4.5. Pro: Immigrants contribute disproportionately to economic growth in that they are [more likely](https://hbr.org/2016/10/immigrants-play-a-disproportionate-role-in-american-entrepreneurship) than native-born Americans to be entrepreneurs or inventors.

1.3.3.2. Pro: NAFTA has significantly [reduced](https://jackwelch.strayer.edu/winning/border-wall-nafta/) U.S. trade debt to Mexico and Canada.
1.3.4. Con: Trade deficits are not an important consideration in terms of the American economy's health.
1.3.4.1. Pro: Economists are currently in [conflict](https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/nafta-numbers-game-trade-1.4579792) about the accuracy of trade deficit figures due to measurement issues caused by re-exportation.

1.3.4.2.1. Pro: A [growing US economy](https://www.cfr.org/backgrounder/us-trade-deficit-how-much-does-it-matter) can cause a trade deficit as US citizens have more money to buy more goods from abroad.

1.3.4.2.1.1.1. Con: Lots of goods that people buy from abroad are not produced in the US.
1.3.4.2.2. Pro: Trade deficits can be a [result](https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/02/upshot/want-to-rev-up-the-economy-dont-worry-about-the-trade-deficit.html?smid=pl-share&_r=1) of positive economic phenomena such as high demand for American currency.
1.3.4.2.3. Pro: Trade deficits often result from consumers buying imports of better quality or lower cost. Trade deficits indicate that these consumers are having their desires better satisfied as a result of these imports.
1.3.4.3. Pro: A large part of the trade deficit is due to the importing of goods and services by companies. These companies use those imports to run their businesses, employing citizens and paying taxes which boosts the economy.
1.3.4.4. Con: Even if economically beneficial, the trade deficits hurt a [specific segment](https://www.washingtonpost.com/posteverything/wp/2016/05/12/getting-straight-about-the-costs-of-trade/?utm_term=.d095bb41face) of workers who are poorer and therefore deserve consideration above and beyond their GDP contribution.
1.3.5. Con: Only [10%](http://uk.businessinsider.com/canada-mexico-nafta-and-us-trade-deficit-in-charts-2017-10?r=US&IR=T) of America's trade deficit relates to NAFTA trade.
1.3.6. Pro: Running a trade deficit increases the amount of [foreign debt](https://www.theglobaleconomy.com/guide/article/54/) taken on by America. If the deficit outstrips growth it will be unable to fund repayments.
