Discussion Title: Should Referendums Be Abolished?

1. Democracies should not use referendums for policy decisions.
1.1. Con: Decisions taken via referendums tend to be perceived as more legitimate and find more acceptance in the population than decisions derived in other ways.
1.1.1. Pro: People are more willing to accept a result when they feel they had a role in making it.
1.1.1.1. Pro: In group experiments, decisions reached through participatory measures - for example deliberation, but especially voting - had a much higher perceived legitimacy than those without \([Persson, p. 393](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/european-political-science-review/article/effects-of-direct-voting-and-deliberation-on-legitimacy-beliefs-an-experimental-study-of-small-group-decisionmaking/940FC64F77F0AF646852DF16C0C979F0)\).
1.1.1.2. Con: There is evidence that participation in decision-making processes does not necessarily lead to a higher perceived legitimacy of the outcome. Among the factors that can affect the perceived legitimacy is, for example, whether one's participation had a visible effect \([Caripini et al, p. 322f.](http://repository.upenn.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1064&context=asc_papers)\).
1.1.1.3. Con: People also participate in elections, and thus indirectly have a role in all political decisions, even those made by politicians.
1.1.2. Pro: Referendums provide an important check on political decision-making between elections by ensuring that key changes only take place with popular support.
1.1.3. Con: Referendums are unable to capture the complexity of [non separable preferences](https://academic.oup.com/pan/article-abstract/9/2/95/1548180/Nonseparable-Preferences-Measurement-Error-and), which many voters might have. Therefore referendums may produce unstable collective choices that are opposed by a significant amount of voters \([Lacy/Niou, p.26](https://sites.duke.edu/niou/files/2011/06/referendum-JTP.pdf)\).
1.1.4. Pro: The people are the final source of legitimacy in a democracy. Thus, decisions made by them have greater legitimacy than decisions made for them by representatives.
1.1.5. Con: More 'legitimacy' can lead to the dogmatic believe that what the majority of voters voted for is the \(only\) right choice while this 'legitimacy' is solely based on an 'ad populum' fallacy.
1.1.5.1. Con: Democracy is based on a government acting out the will of the people. If the majority of the inhabitants want something that is "wrong" than the government should still do it. That is not an 'ad populum' fallacy but how the government should work.
1.1.6. Con: Referendums do not enable compromises, as only one side wins. This can lead to a political division of a country.
1.1.6.1. Pro: The ["Brexit" referendum](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2016/jun/24/britain-votes-for-brexit-eu-referendum-david-cameron) created animosity between supporters and opposers.
1.1.6.1.1. Pro: During the campaign in the run-up to the referendum, Remain campaigners reported being called [traitors and liars](http://www.zeit.de/politik/ausland/2016-06/brexit-abstimmung-strassenwahlkaempfer-grossbritannien/seite-2) while canvassing in London, or not daring to openly wear Remain stickers in the North of England, where support for Brexit was high.
1.1.6.1.2. Pro: Some observers argue that the hateful rhetoric employed during the campaign divided the country so deeply that some people felt empowered to use violence, as evidenced by the [murder of MP Jo Cox](https://www.nytimes.com/2016/06/17/opinion/jo-cox-brexit-and-the-politics-of-hate.html), who supported Remain.
1.1.6.2. Pro: The search for compromise, tedious as it may be, has the potential to satisfy more stakeholders and overcome partisanship, neither of which referendums can achieve.
1.1.6.2.1. Pro: Referendums cannot create compromise because they only ask for approval and rejection. Political philosophers like Max Weber therefore [rejected them](http://gutenberg.spiegel.de/buch/parlament-und-regierung-im-neugeordneten-deutschland-8127/7) as an element of democratic governance; he saw compromises as the foundation of most laws in modern states.
1.1.6.2.1.1. Con: There are plenty of examples of referendums that offered more than two binary options to voters \([Emerson, p. 163](https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=JQ5C5nazfjEC&pg=PA163&dq=%22The+world%27s+first+multi-option+vote%22+Emerson&hl=en&sa=X&ei=G2cQT4yNCMeyhAfDteWGAg&redir_esc=y#v=onepage&q=%22The%20world's%20first%20multi-option%20vote%22%20Emerson&f=false)\).
1.1.6.2.1.1.1. Con: Referendums that offer more than two binary options could be [easily misused](https://www.standardmedia.co.ke/article/2000008414/multiple-choice-referendum-would-be-open-to-manipulation) to influence the outcome by confusing voters.
1.1.6.2.1.1.1.1. Pro: There is a lot of [controversy](http://www.huffingtonpost.com/julio-pabon/statehood-vote-in-puerto-_b_2094586.html) about the 2012 Puerto Rico referendum. It has been argued that the only reason for more options on the referendum was to reach the outcome that the government wanted: statehood.
1.1.6.2.1.1.1.2. Con: Binary referendums can be equally manipulative, for example by wording their question in certain ways, as [was the case](https://www.bloomberg.com/view/articles/2016-07-05/hungary-s-manipulative-referendum) in the Hungarian referendum on refugees in 2016.
1.1.6.2.1.1.2. Con: Even referendums with more than two options are usually able to capture only part of the complexity of many issues.
1.1.6.2.1.1.3. Con: It does not matter how many options a referendum contains: In the end, only one side wins and the others lose.
1.1.6.2.1.1.3.1. Pro: Simple maths argues that multiple options will split votes. Even if the options are balanced the voters could well end up voting in minority for one option that is contested by a majority of voters who split their votes yet the minority option still prevails.
1.1.6.2.1.1.4. Con: New Zealand used a multiple referendum process to debate having a new flag. This move has been widely attacked for not delivering options the voters would have actually supported. [Results show that the flag referendum was a 'major failure'.](http://www.newshub.co.nz/home/politics/2016/03/results-show-flag-referendum-a-major-failure---opposition.html)
1.1.6.2.1.1.5. Pro: Sweden held a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_nuclear_power_referendum,_1980) in 1980 in which multiple policies regarding the country's nuclear power plants were proposed: two in favor of nuclear power, one against.
1.1.6.2.1.1.6. Pro: New Zealand decided in 1992 through a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_reform_in_New_Zealand#1992_electoral_system_referendum) \(that was non-binding\) about its future election system. Voters could decide whether to replace the current system, and if they wanted to do so, between four alternatives.
1.1.6.2.1.1.6.1. Con: Even so, what sounded appealing on paper to the electorate has not necessarily translated into a system the electorate then embraces. NZ opted for the MMP \(Mixed Member Proportional\) system but [voter participation has been on decline despite this.](https://www.radionz.co.nz/news/political/322503/voter-participation-on-decline-despite-mmp)
1.1.6.2.1.1.7. Pro: Newfoundland decided in 1948 through a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newfoundland_referendums,_1948#Referendums) about its political future in relations to Canada and Great Britain, and whether it wanted to be independent from the two.
1.1.6.2.1.1.8. Pro: A decision about Finland's prohibition of alcohol was made in 1931 through a [referendum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_prohibition_referendum,_1931). Voters were able to choose from complete prohibition, prohibition with exceptions for weak drinks and the abolition of prohibition.
1.1.6.2.1.1.9. Pro: The people of Puerto Rico decided in a [referendum in 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rican_status_referendum,_2012) about the territory's political status. Voters who decided in a first question for a change of the political status could then choose from three options: statehood within, independence from or free association with the United States.
1.1.6.2.1.2. Con: There are a number of issues that are just binaries e.g. whether or not same sex marriage should be legal, whereby compromising or finding a middle ground is impossible. This is true regardless of whether these issues are decided by referendum or traditional representative structures.
1.1.6.2.1.2.1. Con: Very few issues leave no room for at least some level of compromise. To continue the example of same sex marriage: civil unions have been used as a compromise \(e.g. Civil Partnerships in the UK\), and there are provisions in the Marriage \(Same Sex Couples\) Act that offer assurances to religious groups which represent a non-binary compromise.
1.1.6.2.2. Con: If the ruling party has a governing majority, it is unlikely that they will seek compromise instead of simply pushing through their preferred agenda.
1.1.6.2.2.1. Con: If one ruling party has the absolute majority, compromises are not sought in parliament between the political parties, but inside the faction of the ruling party. Large political parties often consist of many deputies with slightly different attitudes and convictions.
1.1.6.2.2.1.1. Pro: Although the Republicans have the majority in the American Congress, they have a hard time to disestablish Obamacare because of[factional disagreements](http://edition.cnn.com/2017/05/04/politics/health-care-vote/) between moderate and conservative party members.
1.1.6.2.2.1.2. Con: In Australia, the governing Liberal party combined moderate and conservative factions. The direction of the party caused conservative member Cory Bernardi to [quit](http://www.abc.net.au/news/2017-02-07/cory-bernardi-formally-quits-liberal-party/8247402) and form his own party instead of coming to a compromise.
1.1.6.2.2.1.3. Con: Political diversity within majorities, both within governing parties and within coalitions, can lead to extremism rather than compromise, as those from the center of the political spectrum have to appeal to the more extreme forces on either side to gain support.
1.1.6.2.2.1.3.1. Pro: Thus, the only way to achieve compromise is through the opposition party, and if the governing party has a majority, the opposition will lack any influence.
1.1.6.2.2.1.3.2. Pro: For example, [far-right parties in Israel](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2016/may/25/israel-coalition-deal-hard-right-government-avigdor-lieberman) dominate the agenda of the centre-right coalition, and Tea Partiers within Congress push mainstream Republicans to the right on [healthcare](http://edition.cnn.com/2017/05/04/politics/health-care-vote/).
1.1.6.2.2.1.3.3. Con: If someone in the center finds support from both extremes, they will pull both sides closer to the center. If they can only get support from one side, they probably weren't the center.
1.1.6.2.2.2. Con: Most governments are intrinsically reliant on compromises as they base their parliamentary majority on a coalition. As of 2015, [only three](http://www.cityam.com/215578/which-countries-eu-have-left-wingright-wing-governments-and-which-have-coalitions) out of the 27 European democracies were not governed by a coalition of parties.
1.1.6.2.2.2.1. Con: Negotiations with coalition partners do not necessarily result in what would traditionally be seen as "compromise." Rather, they often give disproportionate influence to small parties, who are allowed to monopolize certain areas of policy in exchange for their partnership.
1.1.6.2.2.2.1.1. Pro: In Israel, small far-right parties are often given control over [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naftali_Bennett#Political_career) or [settlement law](http://www.economist.com/news/leaders/21714355-more-choice-good-thing-within-limits-too-many-parties-can-spoil-politics) in exchange for their service as coalition partners.
1.1.6.2.2.3. Pro: Even if a government is based on a coalition, this simply means that two or three parties instead of one can push through their preferred agenda. If the coalition is stable, there is no need for them to engage in compromise with opposition parties.
1.1.6.2.3. Pro: Complex and potentially irreversible decisions are oversimplified by framing issues in the binary and asking voters to choose just one or the other.
1.1.6.2.3.1. Pro: Referendums tend to reduce the decision to yes/no \(in/out, legal/illegal\) options, failing on complex issues like Brexit: [iitm.be](http://iitm.be/changevsbrexit) . The simplification inherent in referenda decision is ideal for populists, who strive at "simple" answers.
1.1.6.2.3.2. Pro: Binary frames leave the public without influence on the implementation of the issue's outcomes.
1.1.6.2.3.2.1. Pro: The Brexit referendum offered almost no guidance on the actual [terms of Brexit.](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2016/jun/24/britain-has-voted-to-leave-the-eu-what-happens-next)
1.1.6.2.3.2.1.1. Pro: -> See 1.1.6.2.3.1.
1.1.6.2.3.2.2. Con: At least referendums enable every voter to contribute to the initial decision before implementation. This still enables more participation than a political system without referendums, where those decisions are made by the tiny minority of people who are in government.
1.1.6.2.3.2.2.1. Con: The problem with this is that it gives an illusion of legitimacy to the decision. In actual fact, the politicians still have all the power of implementation.
1.1.6.2.3.2.3. Con: This problem exists with or without direct democracy. In both scenarios, politicians have to implement the policy.
1.1.6.2.3.2.4. Con: This is potentially a problem of there not being enough referendums rather than there being too many. If voters were also asked to vote on the details of Brexit this would have allowed a more nuanced answer on what Brexit actually should mean.
1.1.6.2.3.2.4.1. Con: If multiple referendums were necessary to determine the intricacies of policy, it would be unmanageable and unworkable.
1.1.6.2.3.3. Pro: Whoever has the power to define the question has the capacity to shape its outcome. For instance, there was [great controversy](http://www.bbc.com/news/uk-scotland-scotland-politics-21245701) about the wording of the question and the options for the 2014 Scottish Independence Referendum.
1.1.6.2.3.4. Con: -> See 1.1.6.2.1.1.
1.1.6.2.4. Con: Even if this is true, it is sometimes the case that compromise simply cannot be reached, and in those cases a referendum would be preferable to a ruling party imposing a unilateral decision that is outside its mandate.
1.1.6.3. Con: In politics, it is always true that one side wins. The implementation of policies allows for some concessions to be made which can still happen with referendums.
1.1.6.3.1. Con: This may not be the case when, for example, slim victories are interpreted as a mandate for extreme implementations, as is the case with the demand for a "[hard Brexit.](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2017/jan/15/theresa-may-uk-is-prepared-to-accept-hard-brexit)"
1.1.7. Pro: When the majority vote is decisive, it gives the government a clear mandate to act in accordance with the referendum decision.
1.1.7.1. Pro: If the political opposition attacks a government policy that is based on a referendum, the government can refer to the popular vote to justify its decision.
1.1.7.2. Pro: Unsatisfied citizens can more easily protest against politicians' decisions and accuse them of illegitimacy than against decisions made by the people.
1.1.7.3. Pro: In democratic systems, political legitimacy is derived from [democratic approval](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/legitimacy/#PubReaDemApp). A referendum decision expresses such democratic approval, which renders corresponding policy decisions politically legitimate and delegitimises critics.
1.1.7.4. Con: The same could be said of decisive victories in national elections.
1.1.7.5. Con: This assumes the vote is decisive. Given that referendums are often controversial, they frequently lead to quite close votes \(see [Brexit](http://www.bbc.com/news/politics/eu_referendum/results), [Australian republic](http://www.aec.gov.au/Elections/referendums/1999_Referendum_Reports_Statistics/Key_Results.htm)\).
1.1.8. Con: If participation in a referendum is low its result can be seen as illegitimate.
1.1.8.1. Pro: The [median turnout](http://www.economist.com/news/europe/21699146-direct-democracy-spreading-across-europe-not-always-good-thing-referendumania) for nationwide referendums in Europe has fallen from 71% in the early 1990s to 41% in the past years.
1.1.8.1.1. Con: This is correlated heavily with the increasing number of referendums. Arguably, then, this simply reflects the fact that once you have more referendums, the marginal referendums are less important and therefore draw lower turnout, meaning that median turnout is lower. So even though median turnout has fallen, turnout could still be high for major referendums, eg [Brexit](http://www.bbc.com/news/politics/eu_referendum/results).
1.1.8.1.1.1. Pro: Additionally, public opposition to referendums could simply be opposition to these marginal extra referendums, rather than to the concept in general.
1.1.8.2. Pro: After the Brexit referendum, people called [for a second referendum](https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/brexit-petition-second-eu-referendum-latest-news-vote-leave-a7104076.html), referencing low turnout.
1.1.8.3. Con: By this standard, the results of hardly any referendum can be labeled as legitimate in the sense that the majority of eligible citizens support its outcome.
1.1.8.3.1. Pro: Even a referendum like the one on Scottish independence in 2014, which had a [turnout of 85%](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014) and a decisive victory for the no-side, saw only 46% of all registered voters supporting the result.
1.1.8.3.2. Pro: In the case of the Brexit referendum, only [about a third](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016) \(37%\) of eligible voters expressed their support for the result.
1.1.8.4. Con: By this standard, most elected governments are illegitimate as they do not have the expressed support of the majority of citizens eligible to vote.
1.1.8.4.1. Pro: Ever since popular vote numbers have been recorded, [not a single](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_United_States_presidential_elections_by_popular_vote_margin) US President received the support of more than 42% of eligible voters.
1.1.8.4.2. Pro: Even a dominant coalition government like Angela Merkel's, which won [67% of votes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013) in 2013, has the support of only 48% of registered voters.
1.1.8.4.3. Pro: The average member of the German parliament who got their seat through a direct mandate has the support of merely a third of the voters in their respective district \([Vehrkamp, p. 6](http://www.wahlbeteiligung2013.de/fileadmin/Inhalte/Studien/EINWURF_02_2013.pdf)\).
1.1.8.5. Con: In Switzerland, the difference between voter turnout for referendums and elections is marginal. For example, average turnout for national elections is [48.5%](https://www.bfs.admin.ch/bfs/de/home/statistiken/politik.html), while turnout for national referendums is usually [around 45%](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liste_der_eidgen%C3%B6ssischen_Volksabstimmungen).
1.1.8.5.1. Con: Switzerland is a special case: the country has [more than a dozen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_referendums,_2016) referendums in many years, hence not all of them can be expected to be so relevant to large parts of the population that they elicit high turnout.
1.1.8.6. Con: This problem can be solved by introducing a minimum voter turnout for a referendum to be valid.  \([Gay, p. 14](http://researchbriefings.parliament.uk/ResearchBriefing/Summary/SN02809#fullreport)\).
1.1.8.6.1. Pro: This was seen in a 2016 referendum on EU immigration in Hungary, where the results were considered [void](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2016/oct/02/hungarian-vote-on-refugees-will-not-take-place-suggest-first-poll-results) due to a turnout of under 50%, despite 98% of those who turned out voting 'no'.
1.2. Con: Referendums strengthen the voters' position and therefore the foundation of democratic systems.
1.2.1. Con: Referendums are dominated by certain demographic and social groups, thereby giving them disproportional influence over political decisions. Politicians, on the other hand, can try to represent the people as a whole in their decisions.
1.2.1.1. Con: Politicians also typically have to respond to the influence of lobbyists for particular industries or groups that may have funded or backed their campaigns in other ways. This means they can be captured by even narrower interests than referendums.
1.2.1.2. Con: The fact that certain voter groups dominate referendums is hardly an argument against referendums as the alternative, that is: elections, equally suffers from this problem.
1.2.1.2.1. Pro: The voter turnout in Germany's general election in 2013 was higher in areas with a high levels of formal education, high income and little unemployment. Thus, the election's results were "not socially representative anymore" \([SchÃ¤fer et al., p. 4](http://www.wahlbeteiligung2013.de/fileadmin/Inhalte/Studien/Wahlbeteiligung-2013-Studie.pdf)\).
1.2.1.2.2. Pro: In American elections during the past decades, voter turnout has [reliably correlated](http://www.electproject.org/home/voter-turnout/demographics) with age \(the older, the more likely to vote\), education \(the more formal education, the more likely to vote\) and race \(Hispanics are less likely to vote\).
1.2.1.2.3. Pro: In the UK's 2015 general election, older people were [much more likely](https://www.ipsos-mori.com/researchpublications/researcharchive/3575/How-Britain-voted-in-2015.aspx) to vote than younger people and voters of a high social class were much more likely to turn out than voters of a low social class.
1.2.1.2.4. Pro: In American elections, the turnout rate of white and black voters is usually above 60%, whereas that of Hispanic and Asian voters usually is below 50% \([Brookings. p. 7](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/SOC2016report.pdf)\).
1.2.1.2.5. Pro: Higher turnout by older voters is observed in with [elections as well](http://www.electproject.org/home/voter-turnout/demographics), and is not a phenomenon specific to referendums.
1.2.1.2.5.1. Con: Older people are more likely to turn out because they have more time - i.e. are less likely to be working on election day, and are more stable in their jobs \(and so more able to take time off to vote if they are working\). When elections are relatively rare, younger people are more able to make the effort to get to the polls - regular referendums quickly exhaust their ability to do so.
1.2.1.2.5.1.1. Con: In many countries, elections and referendums are held [on weekends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Election_day) to ensure that a maximum number of citizens are able to participate.
1.2.1.2.5.2. Con: At least in the context of general elections there will always be a chance for younger voters to change things in a maximum of five years time. This is very rarely the case with referendums.
1.2.1.2.5.3. Pro: -> See 1.2.1.2.3.
1.2.1.2.5.4. Pro: -> See 1.2.1.2.2.
1.2.1.3. Pro: Voters from the working class are less likely to vote than voters who belong to higher classes.
1.2.1.3.1. Pro: In the case of the Brexit referendum, there was a [correlation](http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/2016/06/24/how-did-turnout-affect-the-eu-referendum-result/) between social class and voter turnout, with people belonging to lower classes being less likely to vote.
1.2.1.4. Pro: Older people are significantly more likely to vote in referendums than younger people.
1.2.1.4.1. Pro: In the Brexit referendum of 2016, elderly people were much more likely to vote and thus over-represented. Depending [on the](http://www.newstatesman.com/politics/staggers/2016/06/how-did-different-demographic-groups-vote-eu-referendum) [source](https://twitter.com/stephen501/status/746806362986975233), the turnout of voters aged 55-64 was 2.3 to 2.9-times as high as that among voters aged 18-24.
1.2.1.4.1.1. Con: Turnout for the referendum was [higher than the last national election](http://www.bbc.com/news/politics/eu_referendum/results), so the result was arguably more representative of the public's will than the government is.
1.2.1.4.1.2. Con: Youth turnout in the Brexit referendum was [higher](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2016/jul/09/young-people-referendum-turnout-brexit-twice-as-high) [than](http://www.if.org.uk/archives/6576/how-high-was-youth-turnout-at-the-2015-general-election) it was in recent national elections.
1.2.1.4.2. Con: -> See 1.2.1.2.5.
1.2.1.4.3. Pro: According to surveys, voter turnout in the referendum on Scottish independence in 2014 was generally higher in older age brackets \(except for those aged 16-17\). Turnout among those in their 60s was 96% compared with 54% among those aged 18-24 \([Electoral Commission, p. 11](http://www.electoralcommission.org.uk/__data/assets/pdf_file/0005/179807/Scottish-referendum-Public-Opinion-survey-ICM-Report-WEBSITE.pdf)\).
1.2.1.5. Con: While the demographics of the participation in referendums might not always be representative, all voters have at least the chance to participate. This is not the case for decisions made by parliament.
1.2.1.5.1. Con: All citizens have their chance to participate by voting at each election.
1.2.1.5.1.1. Con: This is a much more indirect form of participation than direct input on the topic being discussed.
1.2.1.6. Con: Politicians are not necessarily interested in representing the nation as a whole but, primarily, their constituents who vote for them. Their decisions can therefore equally be dominated by considerations of some demographic groups depending on the demographic composition of their constituents.
1.2.1.6.1. Pro: Some members of the US Congress [represent](http://proximityone.com/cd113_demographics.htm) an electoral district with a median annual income of 110.000$, others with a medium annual income of less than 24.000$.
1.2.1.6.2. Pro: Some members of the US Congress [represent](http://proximityone.com/cd113_demographics.htm) an electoral district where almost 70% of people have at least a Bachelor's degree, others where only 8% have one.
1.2.1.6.3. Pro: The vast majority of electoral districts in the East of Germany are far less rich than the average electoral district and face shrinking populations dominated by men. None of these problems are present in the electoral districts in the country's South \([Berlin Institut, p. 27, 29, 32](http://www.berlin-institut.org/fileadmin/user_upload/Die_demografische_Lage_2011/D-Engagement_online.pdf)\).
1.2.1.6.4. Pro: Many political decisions affect people in certain areas in different ways than people in other areas, hence the representatives of these people can hardly be expected to represent the nation as a whole.
1.2.1.6.4.1. Pro: In Germany, for example, the search for a nuclear waste depository has been [dragging on for decades](http://www.spiegel.de/international/germany/the-curse-of-gorleben-germany-s-endless-search-for-a-nuclear-waste-dump-a-672147.html), in part because no one wants it in their vicinity.
1.2.2. Pro: In countries that allow for legislative initiatives via referendums, legislators or other policy-makers often respond to the threat of referendums by being more responsive to popular demands.
1.2.2.1. Pro: There are a number of laws in the US that were passed due to pressure from citizens filing legislative initiatives, including laws making funding available for Medicaid, and restricting water pollution caused by toxic chemicals  \([Schmidt 1989, p. 26](https://books.google.co.uk/books/about/Citizen_Lawmakers.html?id=3L6kQgAACAAJ&redir_esc=y)\)
1.2.2.2. Con: Politicians already have to act according to popular demand as they can be voted out at the next election. There is equal ability to ignore these threats under both scenarios.

Pro: [Deliberative democracy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/deliberative-democracy) is an approach that is superior to referendums because it typically attempts to reach consensus through consultation at every stage of the process, whereas the majoritarian focus of referendums has no such aims and is necessarily polarising.
1.9.1.1.1. Pro: A deliberative model of democracy [stresses the importance of voice.](http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379415000384) Referendums, on the contrary, prioritize votes.
1.9.1.1.1.1. Con: Deliberative processes are usually [biased in favour of powerful agents](https://www.ucalgary.ca/peacestudies/files/peacestudies/young-day-2.pdf) and risk entrenching existing power structures in society that e.g. afford more credibility to some participants based on their gender or way of speaking. While not as nuanced, simple voting processes treat every participant equally.
1.9.1.1.2. Pro: In 2012, the Irish government established a [constitutional convention](https://www.constitution.ie/Convention.aspx), a body made up of ordinary citizens, to deliberate on and recommend changes to the constitution. This process was generally considered to be [a success.](https://politicalreform.ie/2014/02/26/the-irish-constitutional-convention-completes-its-work/)
1.9.1.1.3. Pro: Theorists of [sortition](http://www.irishtimes.com/news/politics/let-s-reimagine-democracy-replace-elections-with-lotteries-1.2870438) argue that bodies of citizens, selected randomly and frequently by lot, should be empowered to make important political decisions. This would function similarly to jury duty, but on a much larger scale.
1.9.1.1.3.1. Con: Just like juries, these bodies would risk perpetuating existing [structures of discrimination](http://www.ncsc-jurystudies.org/~/media/Microsites/Files/CJS/What%20We%20Do/Elek%20%20Hannaford-Agor%202015%20-%20Implicit%20bias%20and%20the%20American%20juror.ashx), especially since they cannot be held accountable for discriminating behaviour like politicians can.
1.9.1.1.4. Pro: A model which allows citizens to contribute at every stage of the policy making process rather than just the end is more democratic.
1.9.1.1.4.1. Pro: -> See 1.2.5.
1.9.2. Con: Frequent referendums lead to voter fatigue and make elections seem less important, thereby [harming voter turnout.](https://editorialexpress.com/cgi-bin/conference/download.cgi?db_name=RESConf2016&paper_id=1009)
1.9.2.1. Pro: Although Switzerland holds [more referendums](http://www.radioaustralia.net.au/international/2014-09-18/6-things-you-need-to-know-about-referendums/1369335) than any other country, its voter turnout in elections \(at around 50% or less\) is among the lowest in the OECD and has been low for decades \([OECD, p. 97](http://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/docserver/download/8111041ec029.pdf?expires=1486943091&id=id&accname=guest&checksum=705611406CD6C9EC244F6B9C3BE4B9BB)\).
1.9.2.1.1. Pro: Researchers have established a persistently negative correlation between referendums and the participation in elections for the case of Switzerland \([Freitag/ Stadelmann-Steffen, p. 479](http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S026137941000048X)\).
1.9.2.1.2. Pro: Voter fatigue and the perception that individual elections do not matter have been proposed as the link between the high number of referendums and the low voter turnout in Switzerland \([Blais, p. 526](http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/spsr.12116/abstract); [Franklin, p. 98](https://books.google.co.kr/books?id=plYRx0sFT-cC&printsec=frontcover&dq=Voter+Turnout+and+the+Dynamics+of+Electoral+Competition+in+established+Democracies+Since+1945&hl=en&sa=X&redir_esc=y#v=onepage&q=Voter%20Turnout%20and%20the%20Dynamics%20of%20Electoral%20Competition%20in%20established%20Democracies%20Since%201945&f=false)\).
1.9.2.1.3. Con: The low voter turnout in Switzerland has not only been linked to issues relating to the country's frequent referendums, but also the "extraordinary complexity of the political system" \([Blais, p. 526](http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/spsr.12116/abstract)\).

1.14.3.3.2.3.2. Pro: There are many instances where vested interests can manifest themselves very subtly - for example, when the company with a vested interest is a donor to the news site publishing the article. Vested interests in these cases may be difficult for the average person to detect.
