Discussion Title: Should governments make an effort to reduce the gender pay gap?

1. Efforts should be made by the government to reduce the gender pay gap.
1.1. Con: The gender pay gap is a natural phenomenon due to individual choices and strengths. As such, the government should not intervene.
1.1.1. Con: An [in-depth analysis](https://hired.com/gender-wage-gap) of more than 100,000 job offers in the US found that 63% of the time, men receive higher salary offers than women for the same job title at the same company.
1.1.1.1. Con: This analysis does not control for total compensation, age, experience, location or other factors - it just looks at men vs women for same job salaries.
1.1.1.2. Con: The website asserts that 63% of the time, men receive higher salary offers. The website does not reveal how often women receive higher offers, and by withholding this information, the website makes its claims appear more dramatic. The implication of not showing that data is that only men receive higher offers. However, women may have received higher offers in that study up to 37% of the time. A more forthcoming review of the data would provide a better support for the point.
1.1.1.2.1. Con: It would not make logical or intuitive sense for women to be receiving higher offers than men, en masse, and turning them down in favour of less pay. That is probably why the study refrained from including those data-points, as the prior suggests it is incredible improbable.
1.1.1.2.1.1. Con: This is because you don't "receive" an offer you negotiate an offer. Studies have shown men \(in general\) are more likely to negotiate for higher pay. Women tend to preference more vacation and flexible hours.
1.1.1.3. Con: There are serious flaws in the methods used in that analysis, making it close to meaningless. It seems to completely ignore variables that can have a substantial impact on wages offered, such as former work experience in a related field, variations in education and other factors.
1.1.2. Pro: As a result of biological differences, men are capable of completing more jobs than women - e.g. jobs that require heavy lifting.
1.1.2.1. Con: Jobs that require heavy lifting aren't well paid at all, neither in the metaphorical, nor in the literal sense. Therefore, this can't explain the pay gap.
1.1.2.2. Con: Equating every workers value on their ability to lift heavy things also discriminates against weak men and women, the otherly abled, and older workers. It is also a random marker for what should be valued in an employee or gender or person. We could pick any random charcteristic that applies to a sex and make that the marker. Ie: birthing ability is a feat of strength, why not use that as a marker? "Lifting up heavy things" suits a patriarchy society that doest want to share with women.
1.1.2.3. Con: While there may be physical differences between the average man and the average woman, there is significant overlap between the two genders \(e.g. in [strenght](http://www.warandgender.com/wggendif.htm)\). This means that differences between individuals are much less significant than averages imply.
1.1.2.3.1. Con: If a task requires an above-average amount of strength or speed, the position will be filled by an individual in the upper percentile of strength or speed and will disproportionately consist of males \(i.e. professional athletes, [coal miners](https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&rct=j&q=&esrc=s&source=web&cd=24&cad=rja&uact=8&ved=0ahUKEwiD_MHX8-3WAhWIKCYKHYv_C2I4FBAWCDowAw&url=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.chamberofmines.org.za%2Findustry-news%2Fpublications%2Ffact-sheets%2Fsend%2F3-fact-sheets%2F424-women-in-mining&usg=AOvVaw1ucQEfPdyyZANkGRnTPwfw), lumberjacks, [firefighters](http://www.nytimes.com/2000/02/03/nyregion/despite-recruiting-few-women-do-well-in-firefighter-tests.html)\).
1.1.3. Pro: Men have a primary social purpose of economic [gain](http://articles.latimes.com/2013/dec/26/local/la-me-one-way-gender-revolution-20131227), so the gender pay gap better facilitates that basic goal.
1.1.3.1. Con: Telling all men that their primary social function is to labor effectively condemns them to servitude.
1.1.3.2. Con: There is no inherent or logical reason why this always has to be the case. Just because historically societies have assigned gender roles as such does not justify it being so.
1.1.3.2.1. Pro: And such notions of people, not just men, existing for "economic gain" are historically quite recent being born largely out of the industrial revolution and the need to drive subsistence farmers off of common lands and into factories.
1.1.4. Pro: The gender pay gap is often conflated with the unadjusted pay gap. When all variables other than gender are controlled for, such as hours worked, occupation, education, the pay gap disappears.
1.1.4.1. Pro: This [source](https://www.glassdoor.com/research/app/uploads/sites/2/2016/03/Glassdoor-Gender-Pay-Gap-Study.pdf) narrows it down to 5.4%, but fails to account for hours worked. This [source](https://www.jec.senate.gov/public/_cache/files/0779dc2f-4a4e-4386-b847-9ae919735acc/gender-pay-inequality----us-congress-joint-economic-committee.pdf) controls for hours worked, which reduces the pay gap by 6.0 percentage points.
1.1.4.2. Con: The problem lies in the various factors that the unadjusted pay gap controls for. It is in enabling women to work more hours, receive a greater education and aspire to more well-paid occupations that the government should act.
1.1.4.3. Pro: This [report](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/atus_06242016.pdf) finds that among full time, year round workers, men work 8.2 hours a day compared to women's 7.8. This would mean women work 4.5% fewer hours than men.
1.1.4.3.1. Pro: The same study, this time for 2016 instead of 2015, found that accounting for hours worked would reduce the gap by [7.1](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/pdf/atus.pdf) percentage points.

1.1.4.4.1.1. Pro: [Risk-taking](https://hbr.org/2015/04/not-taking-risks-is-the-riskiest-career-move-of-all) has been found to be a crucial part of having a successful career and getting senior jobs.
1.1.4.4.2. 1.1.4.5. Pro: Reducing the wage gap would require women to work more hours. This would not be fair to women.
1.1.4.6. Pro: [Uber's data](http://fortune.com/2018/02/06/uber-gender-pay-gap-study/) on the subject illustrates that the 7% pay gap amongst their employees is due to factors other than gender discrimination.

1.1.6. Con: Placing all the blame on individual preferences ignores the discrimination women have been facing for centuries that might impact their ability to gain respect, promotions, or raises in the workplace.
1.1.6.1. Con: Discrimination impacting a woman's ability to gain promotions and raises... etc would result in the gender wage gap, a difference in the hourly wage of woman compared to men. As this is illegal, the government already prevents it, meaning that it could not be the cause of the gender pay gap. Thus the cause of the gender pay gap is in fact likely individual preference.
1.1.7. Pro: Providing equality of opportunity can be better suited for government intervention in developed economies and societies, i.e countries where equality of the sexes is agreed and desired by most of the population.
1.1.8. Con: As the paygap [changes](http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2018/04/09/gender-pay-gap-facts/) over [time](https://iwpr.org/last-decade-slowest-progress-wage-gap/) it proves that it is not a "natural" side-effect in society, but an artificial phenomenon.
