Discussion Title: Time for a Maximum Wage? Should the US Limit CEO Pay?

1. US companies should be prohibited from paying any employee more than 35 times the compensation of their lowest-paid employee.
1.1. Con: The existence of highly paid, wealthy executives is beneficial to society.
1.1.1. Pro: High paid business leaders often start or invest in new companies which fill some large need or want that the society has.  As one example, Elon Musk would not have had 10 million to invest in safe AI research if his lowest paid employee would have then needed at least a $285,715.00 salary.  Comparable arguments could be made for Tesla Motors, Space X, Solar City, and the rest of his companies and investments.
1.1.1.1. Con: There's a difference between entrepreneur founder/CEOs like Musk and non-founder CEOs, who attain their wealth without the risk borne by founder CEOs.
1.1.1.2. Con: Elon Musk, like most wealthy entrepreneurs, made his fortune primarily through [sales of equity](http://time.com/money/4883868/8-innovative-ways-elon-musk-made-money-before-he-was-a-billionaire/), not pay.
1.1.2. Pro: Reducing executive pay would result in a loss of tax revenue.
1.1.2.1. Pro: Since higher brackets of income are taxed at increasingly high rates, every marginal dollar of high income is comparatively more valuable to the government.
1.1.2.1.1. Pro: A salary cap as proposed here would primarily hit those with an income of over $250.000 per year. [This group](http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2016/04/13/high-income-americans-pay-most-income-taxes-but-enough-to-be-fair/) currently includes only about 3% of tax payers yet accounts for more than half of all income tax paid - which equals [about a quarter](http://www.cbpp.org/research/policy-basics-where-do-federal-tax-revenues-come-from) of all federal government revenue.
1.1.2.2. Con: It is possible that companies will instead raise wages across the board in order to prop up CEO pay, meaning both more tax revenue from people with now higher wages, as well as maintaining the current \(or close to\) level of tax collection.
1.1.2.2.1. Con: This policy necessitates an across the board pay increase in order to facilitate an increase in executive pay. For most firms, this would be far more expensive than just increasing the pay of executives. This means that it would be much less likely to be backed by the board or shareholders.
1.1.2.3. Pro: The government could avoid the loss of revenue and [still combat inequality](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2017/jan/10/jeremy-corbyn-maximum-pay-panel) by heavily taxing CEOs and top earners through re-distributive progressive taxation, thereby reducing inequality while increasing revenue.
1.1.2.3.1. Pro: Increasing progressive tax rates would reduce income inequality.
1.1.2.3.1.1. Con: Using the tax system to exert downward pressure on CEO pay has proven ineffective.
1.1.2.3.1.1.1. Pro: In 1993, Congress capped the corporate tax deduction for senior managers' pay at $1 million, but companies merely [deferred](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2017/03/03/uncapping-executive-pay/) payment until the executives retired.
1.1.2.3.1.2. Pro: Increasing progressive tax rates would reduce inequality while being less disruptive to business, because the government would be working within an existing and well-understood framework \(taxation\) rather than creating an unprecedented new type of regulation.
1.1.2.3.1.3. Pro: Some academics have [proposed](https://www.nytimes.com/2014/04/13/business/better-insurance-against-inequality.html?ref=business) indexing tax rates for inequality such that tax rates would become more progressive as inequality increased, ensuring that the super-rich would bear a greater tax burden, while also alleviating the burden on the less wealthy.
1.1.2.4. Con: Given that part of the purpose of tax revenue is to enable the government to provide services, raising wages for people, especially at the bottom, reduces the demand for those services and thus the money needed to fund them.
1.1.2.4.1. Pro: Spending on safety net programs for low income individuals and families currently accounts for [about 10%](http://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-budget/policy-basics-where-do-our-federal-tax-dollars-go) of the federal budget.
1.1.2.4.2. Pro: Companies like Walmart pay their employees so little that many of them qualify for food stamps, Medicaid and other taxpayer-funded assistance programs. For Walmart alone, higher wages would [save the government billions](https://americansfortaxfairness.org/files/Taxpayers-and-Walmart-ATF1.pdf).
1.1.2.4.3. Pro: If enforcement were by taxing income in excess of the cap at 100%, then companies would be forced into a choice: pay their upper management excessively, volunteering that money to be taxed; raise lower-level employee wages or reduce product costs, both freeing up more money for more people to spend on their own other needs; or invest that money into other services, which will likely include public service at various places throughout the economy.
1.1.2.4.3.1. Con: A fifth option companies might choose is to hang onto all that surplus money in the name of the company rather than any individual, as a nest egg for fines, allowing them greater leeway to ignore regulations of any sort.
1.1.2.5. Con: As the CEO of a company; they have several other "non-income" methods for receiving funds from their company, such as dividends. They can also spend funds on company assets that remain under their control keeping them "under" the 35x cap \( such as company boats, company mansions, etc.\).
1.1.2.6. Pro: By sub contracting menial labour companies relieve themselves of the duty ["of having to pay a full-time salary"](http://www.hcr-llc.com/blog/7-reasons-why-subcontracting-may-be-right-for-you). This leaves the worker without a dependable income, and avoids paying fair taxes.
1.1.2.6.1. Con: In some instances, the use of sub-contracting costs more than employing labour. The contractor must be paid, and the workers are less used to the specific circumstances of the company making them less effective. If the CEO sub-contracts in their own self-interest in these cases, this will act against the company's interests.
1.1.2.6.2. Con: These salary caps could just be written into law such that the lowest paid employee is defined to include contracted workers.
1.1.2.6.3. Pro: The [Fair Labor Standards Act](http://Fair Labor Standards Act) which [establishes minimum wage, overtime pay, record keeping and child labor standards](http://smallbusiness.chron.com/subcontracting-labor-law-75734.html)  applies only to employees. This means that those hired by contractors for specific jobs do not get the same protections.
1.1.2.6.3.1. Pro: Although [legislative protection](https://fas.org/sgp/crs/misc/R41230.pdf) does exist, such as a requirement to[pay promptly for services given](https://fas.org/sgp/crs/misc/R41230.pdf), the fact that sub contractors work for so many employees makes it impossible to identify which one owes an employer's duty to them.
1.1.2.6.3.1.1. Pro: This means that it is unlikely that sub contractors could ever be given the same legal protections as regular employees are now. Thus, pushing employees to use sub contracted labour necessarily means that those now working will get less protection.
1.1.2.6.4. Pro: Subcontractors, like janitorial services companies for example, are much more likely to employ their workers on the basis of client demand which requires that contracts that have flexible hours and do not promise a given number of hours each week.These contracts tend to be abusive.
1.1.2.6.4.1. Pro: These kinds of contracts can include abusive "[at-will contracts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/At-will_employment)" or "[zero-hour contracts](http://www.bbc.com/news/business-23573442)" which make both pay and hours extremely inconsistent and destroy financial security for entry-level workers.
1.1.2.6.4.2. Pro: Flexible hours tend to require voluntary or involuntary on-demand work, where an employer can call an employee at any time of day and ask that they work. This sort of work puts chronic stress on individuals who never know when they might get a call.
1.1.2.6.4.3. Con: These contracts allow domestic workers to be more competitive and helps prevent outsourcing, lay-offs, and automation.
1.1.2.6.4.4. Pro: These contracts coerce workers into accepting unethical working conditions because employees fear speaking out when their employment \(or number of hours of work\) can be terminated or cut down at any time.
1.1.2.6.4.5. Pro: These sorts of contracts increase inequality because workers are unlikely to spend money on fixed cost commodities \(ie an education or mortgage\) - which are especially important at increasing social mobility - when these individuals are unsure of how much work they will get in any given week.
1.1.2.6.4.5.1. Con: However, these contracts are often chosen by workers who  need a flexible work arrangement, such as college students, to help meet the costs of their education.
1.1.2.6.4.5.2. Pro: A decrease in spending by low-income individuals is also likely to hurt the economy which is reliant on steady consumption.
1.1.2.6.4.6. Pro: Contracts which allow for flexible hours allow for companies to hire many more workers than they actually need and then give each of them relatively few hours. This allows these companies to avoid providing certain rights or protections, such as notice before firing or healthcare, that are afforded on the basis of how many hours individuals work.
1.1.2.6.4.7. Con: Subcontractors and the contracts they use allow the economy to be more flexible. During Christmas, for instance, individuals can work at Macy's when they have far more consumer demand, then move to another job after the season is over. This maximizes efficiency and worker pay.
1.1.2.7. Pro: When Switzerland was debating the introduction of a 12:1 salary cap, this policy was forecast to lead to major financial losses.
1.1.2.7.1. Pro: A study estimated that, even if the salaries cut at the top are paid to other workers, the country would lose 0.5 to 1 Billion Francs in tax revenue. Additionally, up to a billion could be lost in social security contributions. Under all scenarios the bottom line would be negative \([Keuschnigg at al., p. 8-10](http://www.swiss-economics.ch/RePEc/files/S0009SGV.pdf)\).
1.1.2.7.1.1. Pro: These losses would be even larger under the assumption that some companies and workers would leave the country. The annual losses in tax revenue and social security payments could then exceed 2-3 Billion \([Keuschnigg at al., p. 8-10](http://www.sgv-usam.ch/fileadmin/user_upload/1_zu_12/de/20130910_mk-studie/20130910_mk-1zu12-studie_studie-lang_de.pdf)\).
1.1.2.8. Con: Excessively compensated employees "continue, however, to deduct the [majority of their executive compensation](http://www.epi.org/publication/taxes-executive-compensation/), with these deductions costing the U.S. Treasury an estimated $7.5 billion per year."
1.1.3. Pro: High paid business leaders often utilize their wealth for philanthropic efforts.
1.1.3.1. Con: In the US, individuals with high income [give a smaller share](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2013/04/why-the-rich-dont-give/309254/) of their incomes to charity than do those with low income. The middle class is the [most generous](https://www.philanthropy.com/article/America-s-Generosity-Divide/156175). Less income inequality might therefore lead to more charitable contributions.
1.1.3.1.1. Con: Nevertheless, the total sum of charitable contributions might be much lower without the mega-donations of rich people.
1.1.3.1.1.1. Con: There is little use considering a scenario that is technically possible, but does not align with prevailing relevant theories, nor match with previous data.
1.1.3.1.1.1.1. Pro: The average propensity to save is positively correlated with income, as people earn more, they save a higher amount of their income.
1.1.3.1.1.2. Pro: Together, the top 50 American donors gave [$5.6 Billion in 2016](https://www.philanthropy.com/article/Phil-Knight-Tops-Chronicle-s/239122) and even $10.2 Billion in 2014 for philanthropic purposes.
1.1.3.1.1.2.1. Pro: These individuals alone gave [about 1.4%](https://givingusa.org/see-the-numbers-giving-usa-2017-infographic/) of the total charitable contributions that Americans made in 2016.
1.1.3.1.1.2.2. Con: These select super-rich individuals are not necessarily representative for Americans who would be affected by a salary cap as a whole.
1.1.3.2. Con: Psychological studies [indicate that](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20649364) individuals of low socio-economic status are more prosocial than those of high social class as they face more risks and have more interest in egalitarian values and compassion.
1.1.3.2.1. Pro: [Other research](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19121143) corroborates this by showing a link between social power and lower distress as well as compassion in the face of other individuals' suffering.
1.1.3.3. Con: The money that rich people give does not necessarily go to public causes and, especially, those in need.
1.1.3.3.1. Pro: Of the [50 largest charitable gifts](https://www.philanthropy.com/factfile/gifts/1?DonorDisplayName_cu=&Category=any&GiftRecipients_RecipOrgDateline_c=&GiftRecipients_RecipStateFull=any&GiftDonors_SourceWealth_cu=&GiftDonors_aStateFull=any&GiftYear=2016) made in the US in 2016, only a single one went to an organization primarily dedicated to social services, and none to organizations that primarily serve education or children and youths in general.
1.1.3.3.2. Pro: [Almost 60%](https://www.philanthropy.com/factfile/gifts/1?DonorDisplayName_cu=&Category=Colleges+and+universities&GiftRecipients_RecipOrgDateline_c=&GiftRecipients_RecipStateFull=any&GiftDonors_SourceWealth_cu=&GiftDonors_aStateFull=any&GiftYear=2016) of the 50 largest charitable gifts made in the US in 2016 went to specific colleges and universities, thus helping many who are already in a good position and even promoting the reproduction of existing elites and rich institutions.
1.1.3.3.3. Con: This is not bad because it allows investment in things which are underappreciated. [The SETI program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Search_for_extraterrestrial_intelligence), for instance, would not exist if not for massive donations by a Russian oligarch.
1.1.3.3.3.1. Con: It should not be a luxury of a small segment of society to determine what things are under-appreciated and benefit from funding while other areas \(including those necessary for basic needs such as education and healthcare\) go underfunded.
1.1.3.4. Con: High paid business leaders often make a show of utilizing their wealth for philanthropic efforts, the amount is often over-inflated, and many times that money isn't being as generously given as it might at first seem.
1.1.3.4.1. Pro: Many 'generous donations' come with [strings attached](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/04/14/your-money/wealth-matters-philanthropy-david-callahan.html?_r=0), such as giving the donor media attention or recognition for the contribution, or allowing the donor to retain control of the expenditure, regardless of whether their spending preferences greatly limits, or even nullifies, the impact of their donation.
1.1.3.4.1.1. Pro: Mark Zuckerberg gave 100 million dollars to education, but after money spent on consultants and related expenses, very little money actually reached the [intended recipients](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/04/14/your-money/wealth-matters-philanthropy-david-callahan.html?_r=0), the under-educated children.
1.1.3.5. Pro: -> See 1.1.3.1.1.2.
1.1.3.6. Pro: Often, certain philanthropic efforts require enormous sums of money to get off the ground. High paid executives can provide this level of capital necessary to achieve success.
1.1.3.6.1. Pro: Few individuals or even philanthropic organizations could finance a university on their own, as George Soros has done with the Central European University in Hungary. It has an endowment by him of [around $800 Million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_European_University).
1.1.3.6.2. Pro: Research Institutes that would otherwise be hard to establish are often propelled by donations of rich individuals, for example the [Huntsman Cancer Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huntsman_Cancer_Institute) which has received more than $250 Million by Jon Huntsman Sr.
1.1.3.7. Con: High earners may give a larger amount to charity, but lower earners give a higher percentage of their income to charity, meaning that the [proportion of charitable funding](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2013/04/why-the-rich-dont-give/309254/) given by high earners is actually lower than their lower earning counterparts.
1.1.3.8. Pro: High earners have more disposable income that they can, and often do, give to charity. This makes the [amount given to charity](https://www.salon.com/2016/12/27/does-being-wealthy-make-you-more-charitable_partner/) by high earners larger than what is given by lower earners.
1.1.3.8.1. Con: -> See 1.1.3.7.
1.1.4. Pro: The prospect of becoming a wealthy executive fuels the American dream and the aspirations of individuals.
1.1.4.1. Pro: The opportunity for promotion and job mobility within a company means that a large pay disparity may act as incentive to workers, as they perceive it as achievable to them.
1.1.4.1.1. Con: Specialisation within a  company means that most employees will never have an opportunity for significant promotion. The staff involved in menial labour are unlikely to be promoted to the role of CEO.
1.1.4.1.2. Pro: Research indicates that forty-eight percent of appointments of managerial positions result from reorganisation within a company. This demonstrates that a degree of mobility does exist with in companies \([Ruderman/Ohlott, p. 7](http://www.ccl.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/RealitiesMgtPromotion.pdf)\).
1.1.4.1.3. Pro: Since career advancement is a [significant motivator](https://www.forbes.com/sites/victorlipman/2014/01/24/want-motivated-employees-offer-ample-opportunities-for-growth/#174ddcd352c6) for employees, there is a strong incentive for companies to allow some form of mobility in the workplace.
1.1.4.1.4. Con: Whilst this is true, a situation where a CEO is paid twenty times, rather than forty times, what the average worker is paid would surely provide enough of a disparity to present an incentive to workers.
1.1.4.2. Pro: Compressing the compensation gap between each management layer within a company will not increase motivation. People are more motivated to work and compete harder if they know the next level up offers a big salary jump.
1.1.4.2.1. Con: There are [numerous](https://www.cnbc.com/2017/08/29/this-boss-set-a-70000-minimum-wage-for-his-employees-3-years-ago.html) [case studies](https://www.inc.com/magazine/201511/paul-keegan/does-more-pay-mean-more-growth.html) that show that when employee pay increases \(especially that of the lowest paid workers\) the value to the company exceeds the expense of increased wages.
1.1.4.3. Pro: If low skill workers are highly compensated, high skill employees may find their compensation is not commensurate with their skill and experience. They may choose to quit and work in easier jobs for the same pay.
1.1.4.3.1. Con: Companies often leave [pay gaps](http://www.workplaceanswers.com/resources/blog/minimum-wage-affects-employees-pay/) between employee rankings to avoid devaluing the employment of more greatly skilled or experienced workers. Under a salary cap, the lowest paid worker will still be paid less than employees on higher rungs of the corporate ladder, however the greatest disparity in salary will be constrained within the 35:1 ratio.
1.1.4.3.2. Pro: If the difference in pay is reduced, less people will be willing to commit to the increased hours, pressure and risk associated with working as a CEO.
1.1.4.3.2.1. Pro: [geolib.com](http://geolib.com/smith.adam/won1-10.html) Here is the section of Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations that addresses how the price of labor is set. Paragraph 24 is a good place to start. Here he explains that those who take on large risks of failure are compensated at a higher rate to compensate for the risk. Furthermore, he describes why those who must be trusted to a greater degree in an occupation earn more. Both of these are true of a CEO.
1.1.4.3.2.2. Pro: High executive pay attracts high quality executives.
1.1.4.3.2.2.1. Pro: CEO's and other high level leaders are paid large salaries as they shoulder the burden of the companies success. If the company fails, the CEO gets blamed, not the lowest level assistant. If leadership can only make a certain amount without raising wages company wide, their incentive to create a growing and successful business goes way down, affecting everyone.
1.1.4.3.2.2.1.1. Con: In practice, CEOs are not held responsible for their failures.
1.1.4.3.2.2.1.1.1. Pro: While the investment banks they ran crashed and collapsed, the top executives of Lehman Brothers and Bear Stearns made more than a billion dollars in the preceding years and left with a profit. This divergence has been linked to incentive structures that reward excessive risk taking \([Bebchuk et al.](http://www.law.harvard.edu/faculty/bebchuk/pdfs/BCS-Wages-of-Failure-Nov09.pdf)\).
1.1.4.3.2.2.1.1.2. Pro: Marissa Mayer, the chief executive of Yahoo, presided over the company's steady decline and absorption by Verizon but made [about 239 Million Dollars](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/03/technology/yahoo-marissa-mayer-compensation.html) over five years, chiefly because the company's financial worth increased and shareholders profited.
1.1.4.3.2.2.2. Con: Even if companies suffer from a salary cap, society as a whole might profit. If executive positions at companies become less attractive, more highly-qualified individuals might choose other areas for work, for example politics or the public sector.
1.1.4.3.2.2.2.1. Pro: Many graduates of top-schools like Harvard, Princeton and Yale usually [end up](https://economix.blogs.nytimes.com/2011/12/21/out-of-harvard-and-into-finance/?_php=true&_type=blogs&_r=0) in high-paying industries like finance and consulting; few enter government service.
1.1.4.3.2.2.2.2. Pro: With the exception of the President, the country's highest public representatives receive a compensation of  [$174,000 per year](https://www.senate.gov/CRSpubs/9c14ec69-c4e4-4bd8-8953-f73daa1640e4.pdf). This is less than [all but three](http://graphics.wsj.com/ceo-salary-vs-company-performance/) CEOs of S&P 500 companies, two of which receive a symbolic salary of only $1. The fourth-lowest paid of these CEOs receives about three times as much as the politicians. This makes high political offices unattractive in comparison.

1.1.4.3.2.2.2.2.3. Con: Political office also enables former presidents the opportunity to earn money as guest speakers at functions for a [fee](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2017/may/01/barack-obama-speaking-fees-economic-racial-justice).
1.1.4.3.2.2.3. Con: High pay fails to secure and retain high quality executives, as CEO turnover continues to rise even as compensation increases.

Whereas before CEOs remained in the one company for long periods, such CEOs are now [dismissed](http://www.nytimes.com/2009/01/04/business/economy/04view.html) and replaced with an alternative candidate.

1.1.4.3.2.2.3.1.1.2. Pro: The performance of executives barely determines their pay; what provides a much better indicator for executives' pay is the size of companies \([Tosi et al, p. 329](http://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/014920630002600207)\).
