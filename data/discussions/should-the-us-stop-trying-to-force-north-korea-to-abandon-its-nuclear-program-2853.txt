Discussion Title: Should the US stop trying to force North Korea to abandon its nuclear program?

1. The US should not try to force North Korea to abandon its nuclear program.
1.1. Con: Formally accepting North Korea as a nuclear state would strengthen the North Korean regime.
1.1.1. Pro: Nothing that increases the power or supports the longevity of the North Korean regime is morally justifiable.
1.1.1.1. Pro: The North Korean regime willingly lets its citizens suffer and [starve regularly](http://www.newsweek.com/north-korea-starving-nuclear-missiles-641188).
1.1.1.2. Con: The North Korean government probably feels the same way about the US. If we are to pursue morals at all costs above law then there is no objective framework for settling disputes internationally.
1.1.1.2.1. Con: The North Korean regime is objectively morally worse than the United States. If we can't call torture and death camps evil, the word has no meaning.
1.1.1.3. Con: Condemning and encouraging the world to act absolves you of moral responsibility if the world fails to act while atrocities happen
1.1.2. Con: This would not represent a significant change to the status quo: the North Korean regime is not under any significant threat from outside or inside forces.
1.1.3. Pro: The suffering people of North Korea deserve an international intervention through, or with the help of, the UN. A nuclear-capable dictator would make this intervention impossible.
1.1.3.1. Pro: North Korea continues to commit every human rights violation except apartheid.
1.1.3.1.1. Pro: North Korea's oppressive [police state](https://www.state.gov/j/drl/rls/fs/2017/273647.htm) commits manifold human rights violations every day.
1.1.3.1.1.1. Con: Some of what are called North Korea's abuses of human rights are common practices in other countries, for example [penal labor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penal_labor_in_the_United_States) in the United States.
1.1.3.1.1.1.1. Con: This does not excuse North Korea.
1.1.3.1.2. Pro: North Korea is a rogue nation, led by psychopathic tyrants that execute people with [mortars](http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/worldnews/asia/northkorea/9630509/North-Korean-army-minister-executed-with-mortar-round.html), imprison people for [three generations](http://www.express.co.uk/news/world/854908/North-Korea-latest-news-children-World-War-3-video-games-Americans), and [abduct foreign citizens](https://qz.com/1078351/dont-forget-about-us-japanese-victims-of-north-koreas-abductions-say/). It is inhabited by a heavily indoctrinated population that is taught [to shoot foreigners while they are still children](http://www.express.co.uk/news/world/854908/North-Korea-latest-news-children-World-War-3-video-games-Americans). Giving them access to nuclear weapons cannot lead to anything good, for anyone.
1.1.3.1.2.1. Con: There are several incosistencies in these statements: psychopathic tyrants wouldn't be able to survive for 60 years while surrounded by American troops and they wouldn't be able to deceive US presidents as they did.
1.1.3.1.2.2. Con: It's obvious that the population is indoctrinated, but how much is this true if people regularly escape from North Korea?
1.1.3.1.3. Pro: In its 2014 report, a UN commission tasked with evaluating the country's human rights record spoke of "systematic, widespread and gross human rights violations " and "crimes against humanity" as essential components of North Korea's political system \([OHCHR, p. 15](http://www.ohchr.org/EN/HRBodies/HRC/CoIDPRK/Pages/CommissionInquiryonHRinDPRK.aspx)\).
1.1.4. Pro: The acceptance of nuclear weapons would be a great success for the regime as this would showcase its ability to stand up to much larger countries.
1.2. Pro: Refusing to accept North Korea's nuclear status and condemning it is ineffective and achieves nothing.
1.2.1. Pro: Condemning NK purely for their nuclear program doesn't accomplish anything and distracts from many other harsh political and human rights violations that they should be condemned for.
1.2.1.1. Con: Condemnation of the nuclear program at least directs attention at North Korea. Otherwise, the world would likely ignore the country and its human rights violations as it isn't of much relevance for any major powers.
1.2.1.1.1. Pro: The human rights violations in Africa, for example, get far less attention--because those countries do not threaten the interests of the powerful nations.
1.2.1.2. Con: We can and should condemn them for all abuses of power.
1.2.2. Pro: Routine condemnation of their nuclear program has proven to be counterproductive and has given North Korea a propaganda tool that the country's government used to further control and oppress its people.
1.2.2.1. Con: The government already controls its people through repression, surveillance, and violence; the issue of propaganda is just symbolic. It is not as if once North Korea can no longer put out propaganda videos, the people will suddenly "wake up" and revolt.
1.2.2.2. Pro: North Korea's entire national identity has revolved around the threat of war with the US, therefore each time we call on NK to end their nuclear program the state run media use this as a rhetorical opportunity to insist the US is trying to weaken the North Korean state.
1.2.2.2.1. Con: It doesn't matter what the US says. The North Korean propaganda arm can and does lie to the populace.
1.2.3. Pro: North Korea cannot be stopped in its development of nuclear weapons anyway.
1.2.3.1. Pro: Despite [strong sanctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanctions_against_North_Korea), the country was able to test nuclear weapons several times.
1.2.3.2. Con: There are alternative approaches to stopping North Korea's nuclear program that better serve American interests.
1.2.3.2.1. Pro: The US could [incentivize](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2016/apr/24/obama-response-north-korea-nuclear-tests-deal) North Korea to stop its nuclear programming by ending or down-scaling its military activity at the North Korean border.
1.2.3.2.1.1. Con: North Korea has offered many times to stop building, but their promises are not credible. The article linked is from 2016, but by the end of [that year](https://www.armscontrol.org/factsheets/dprkchron#2010) the US secretary of state announced that negotiations were a lost cause because Kim Jong Un believed nuclear weapons were necessary for the survival of the DPRK.
1.2.3.2.1.2. Con: Halting American military activity on the border would include the removal of American troops from South Korea, leaving the South Korean military significantly outnumbered in the event of a North Korean land invasion.
1.2.3.2.1.2.1. Con: There is a difference between stopping military exercises that can be interpreted as aggressive in intent and take place close to North Korea's border on the one hand, and outright withdrawing troops on the other hand.
1.2.3.2.1.3. Pro: North Korea commonly interprets the military exercises by the United States and South Korea close to its borders as hostile in intent and presents its aggressive rhetoric and threats [as a defensive reaction](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/10/13/world/asia/north-korea-renews-guam-threat-ahead-of-joint-naval-exercise.html?_r=0).
1.2.3.2.1.4. Pro: Many of the military exercises that involve the US stoke North Korea's worst fears, for example by simulating [decapitation strikes](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2017/aug/11/north-korea-us-south-korea-huge-military-exercise) on the government and its leadership.
1.2.3.2.1.5. Con: Rewarding bad behavior is a perverse incentive. We've seen this pattern before: North Korea extracts concessions, and then cannot be trusted to uphold her end.
1.2.3.2.1.5.1. Con: To the contrary, the staging of massive nuclear exercises close to North Korea's border could be seen as bad behavior that could be stopped as a sign of goodwill without incurring much of a cost.
1.2.3.2.2. Pro: Direct military action can stop the North Korean nuclear weapons program.
1.2.3.2.2.1. Pro: A war with the DPRK now is preferable to a war when the North has a full arsenal of nuclear ICBM's.
1.2.3.2.2.1.1. Con: Among the three options of avoiding war, waging war with terrible consequences and waging war with even more terrible consequences, the preferable one is the first, not the second.
1.2.3.2.2.2. Con: Any military action against the North Korean nuclear weapons program would provoke a large-scale military conflict on the Korean peninsula.
1.2.3.2.2.2.1. Con: If the United States promised nuclear retaliation for any bombardment of Seoul, the North would not fire - even if faced with the destruction of its nuclear program.
1.2.3.2.2.2.1.1. Con: Any attack on nuclear facilities might be interpreted by the North as an all-out war, thus leading to an all-out effort at hitting the enemies South.
1.2.3.2.2.2.1.1.1. Con: If the US clearly informed the North Korean government that we are not interested in regime change and that the bombing and sanctions will stop when KJU agrees to rigorous UN nuclear inspections the risk of misunderstanding US intentions is minimal.
1.2.3.2.2.2.1.1.1.1. Con: North Korea has [learned from the fate of the Gaddafi regime](http://nationalinterest.org/feature/north-korea-has-nuclear-weapons-so-it-wont-end-libya-20060) that it should give up its nuclear weapons under no circumstances and not trust any American assurances that are meant to entice it to do so.
1.2.3.2.2.2.2. Pro: There is no military action that does not lead to the loss of life of hundreds of thousands of South Koreans and Americans living in South Korea.
1.2.3.2.2.2.2.1. Con: -> See 1.2.3.2.2.2.1.
1.2.3.2.2.2.2.2. Pro: North Korean [artillery](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2017/07/the-worst-problem-on-earth/528717/) based north of the DMZ could quickly retaliate against Seoul, leading to horrific casualties.
1.2.3.2.2.2.2.2.1. Con: Assuming Kim Jong Un is a rational actor, he will not fire on Seoul if he knows it will lead to his death and to the end of his regime. If he is not a rational actor then war may be inevitable, and it is better to have it now than to have it when he has a full arsenal of nuclear ICBM's.
1.2.3.2.2.2.2.2.1.1. Con: Game theory suggests he must retaliate even to limited attacks somehow, just to prove he's serious. This would quickly escalate, since the same logic applies to the other side.
1.2.3.2.2.3. Con: Direct military action might draw China and Russia into the conflict.
1.2.3.2.2.3.1. Pro: China would most likely come to North Korea's help if the country were attacked based on the [defense treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-North_Korean_Mutual_Aid_and_Cooperation_Friendship_Treaty) between the two countries that has been in place since 1961.
1.2.3.2.2.4. Pro: An airborne surgical strike on known nuclear facilities would cripple or at least seriously hinder North Korea's nuclear program and force them to the negotiating table.
1.2.3.2.2.4.1. Pro: A bombing campaign would apply pressure until the status quo is less agreeable than negotiation. With bombers we could make KJU's status quo extremely difficult. He might be willing to starve his own people, but when the power goes out in his personal chamber, bunker, or lair it is likely that he will become a great deal more pliable.

Con: It would be next to impossible to hit all nuclear facilities at once, especially as the country is apparently using [mobile launchers](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-northkorea-missiles-china-truck/north-korea-appeared-to-use-china-truck-in-its-first-claimed-icbm-test-idUSKBN19P1J3) for its ICBMs that could be easily hidden.

1.2.3.2.2.4.2.1.1. Con: This would leave North Korea a chance to strike back or lash out in one last effort. Even if this is far from assured, it is a dangerous possibility and makes any strike highly risky.
1.2.3.2.2.4.2.2. Con: An overwhelming nuclear first strike would take out all their mobile launchers at once with high probability.
1.2.3.2.2.4.2.2.1. Pro: And American antimissile systems could defend against a few survivors.
1.2.3.2.2.4.2.3. Con: North Korea doesn't have a working nuclear ICBM yet. But they will soon if no-one stops them.
1.2.3.2.2.4.2.4. 1.2.3.2.2.4.3.1.1. Con: South Korea [has been](http://foreignpolicy.com/2013/04/09/why-doesnt-seoul-have-iron-dome/) trying to purchase the Iron Dome system, even though its appropriateness for the Korean situation has been called into question, and working on as well as purchasing alternative but similar technologies.
